OVERALL

The About section has a clear cinematic ambition — left-to-right slide for the big statement, upward line-by-line reveal for the narrative, a scrub-driven entrance that ties motion to scroll. The structure is right. The critical gap is accessibility: every GSAP animation fires unconditionally, with no check for prefers-reduced-motion. On a recruiter's system with motion reduction enabled, this is a WCAG 2.1 SC 2.3.3 violation and a first impression that breaks before it lands. After that fix, three polish opportunities lift the motion from functional to felt: adding blur to the enter transitions (Jakub's materializing quality), switching to percentage-based slide offsets so the motion scales with the viewport, and giving the scrub timelines a subtle ease curve so the animation feels organic rather than mechanical.

Critical Emil Jakub Jhey

No prefers-reduced-motion guard on any GSAP animation

WhatEvery animation in the useEffect — data-reveal, bigLines slide, SplitText narrative enter, and both exit timelines — runs unconditionally. Users who enable motion reduction (vestibular disorders, migraine, seizure risk) are exposed to the full parallax-scrub experience with no fallback.

Why it mattersWCAG 2.1 SC 2.3.3 (Level AAA) and the project's own PRODUCT.md target WCAG AA with an explicit note to respect prefers-reduced-motion. Beyond compliance, this is a recruiter tool — if a target viewer has this preference set, the first impression is a broken page, not a cinematic one.

Recommended motionRead the preference once at the top of useEffect and skip (or crossfade-only) all GSAP animations when it's set. Use gsap.set() to place elements at their final state immediately, then return. Optionally listen for runtime changes with MediaQueryList.addEventListener('change', ...) so users who toggle the OS setting mid-session are covered.

Important Emil Jhey

Fixed x: -60 pixel offset doesn't scale with viewport

WhatThe bigStatement enter and exit both use x: -60 (absolute pixels). On a 2560px display, -60px is barely 2% of the viewport — nearly imperceptible. On a 375px phone, -60px is 16% of the screen, which may feel too large. The motion isn't anchored to the element's own width or the viewport.

Why it mattersEmil prizes motion that feels native to the element — it should originate from its logical source. Jhey's palette includes CSS custom properties and relative units that make motion scale naturally. A recruiter on an ultrawide monitor sees a barely-perceptible twitch; a mobile reviewer sees a wide sweep. Neither is the intended cinematic slide-in.

Recommended motionReplace x: -60 with xPercent: -100 (slide in from 100% of the element's own width, off-screen to the left). This scales with the element automatically. Pair with overflow: hidden on the parent container so the element doesn't overflow the section during the enter.

Important Jakub

Enter animations lack blur — the "materializing" quality is missing

WhatBoth enter animations (bigLines: x + opacity; SplitText lines: yPercent + opacity) transition only position and opacity. filter: blur() is absent from all enter states.

Why it mattersJakub's signature enter recipe adds a 4–8px blur at the start state that animates to blur(0) as the element enters. This creates a "materializing from thin air" quality — the element doesn't just appear and slide, it resolves into focus. Without it, the enters feel mechanical: you see the element, then it moves. With blur, you don't see the element, then it exists. The difference is subtle but is exactly the gap between "this site has animations" and "this feels expensive."

Recommended motionAdd filter: 'blur(6px)' to the gsap.set() calls for bigLines and SplitText lines, and animate to filter: 'blur(0px)' in the timeline .to(). For scrub animations, ensure the blur resolves cleanly by the end breakpoint. Note: filter is GPU-composited and won't cause layout thrash.

Important Jakub Emil

ease: 'none' in both scrub timelines — motion maps linearly to scroll

WhatBoth scrub timelines (bigLines and SplitText narrative) use ease: 'none' in their .to() calls. In GSAP scrub mode, the easing applied within the timeline controls the shape of the animation curve relative to scroll progress — it's not about speed, it's about how the element "wants" to move as the user scrolls.

Why it mattersease: 'none' produces a perfectly mechanical linear mapping: 50% scroll progress = 50% animation progress. The element doesn't accelerate or settle — it just translates in sync with scroll speed. Jakub's production polish principle is that motion should feel like it has physical weight; power1.inOut gives a gentle ease-in ease-out within the scrub range, making elements feel like they decelerate into their resting position rather than stopping abruptly.

Recommended motionChange ease: 'none' to ease: 'power1.inOut' in both scrub timelines. The user still controls the pace via scroll; the easing controls the curve shape, adding organic feel. Test by scrolling slowly through the section — elements should feel like they settle in, not click into place.
